GovTech a11y Playground

UX/UI Art Direction User Testing 2025

"How can we empower professionals to build more accessible and inclusive digital services?"

During summer 2025, I joined GovTech Singapore as a UX/UI Designer intern. Below are some contributions I made to the a11y Playground team, which I joined halfway through their design process. My role primarily involved setting the site's art direction with illustrations, as well as participating in user interviews and synthesising data for site refinement.

My Contributions

  • Conceptualised and provided reusable illustrations/animations from scratch

  • Presented my design process with 30+ UI/UX Designers during a monthly company huddle

  • Conducted user interviews and synthesised observations and insights, presented findings to team

  • Created and updated Figma frames for the website

    • As a government agency, Govtech has an even greater responsibility to ensure that government digital services meet the needs of users that are People with Disabilities (PWDs). During Inclusive Design Week (IDW) 2025, our team unveiled A11y Playground, an interactive website designed to empower digital practitioners to build more accessible and inclusive digital services in alignment with WCAG guidelines.


    👉 View site here: https://a11y.tech.gov.sg/


    📰 News Coverage: Straits Times, Lian He Zao Bao

    Bridging the Accessibility Gap: Developing a one-stop WCAG-Centred Toolkit for Digital Practitioners

    A11y Playground serves as a comprehensive one-stop toolkit for developers, designers and product managers, to enhance the inclusivity of their work. It bridges the gap between the technical requirements of WCAG and user empathy, ensuring that accessibility is actively integrated into the product lifecycle rather than treated as an afterthought.

    The site consists of these main objectives and features:

    ✏️ Synthesise: Curated WCAG and DSS information into a user-friendly, categorised checklist for real-time site validation.

    ✏️ Empathise: Featuring real-life interviews with PWDs to highlight critical usability gaps and pain points, enabling digital practitioners to better understand their needs.

    ✏️ Prioritise: "People Stories" and "Top 5 common issues" to bring practitioners' attention to the most common site accessibility issues and understand PWD user's pain points.

    ✏️ Equip: Resource page with selected external tools, such as colour-blindness simulators, or design guides for a better implementation of site.

    Setting the Art Direction for a11y Playground

    (Providing 10+ SVG/Lottiefile illustrations from scratch)

    One of the tasks I did was to provide the visual identity for the A11y Playground, creating a cohesive set of illustrations & animations that would represent the brand and it's purpose of creating inclusive digital experiences for everyone.

    Art Direction Process👇

    Creating a strong Brand Identity

    Initially, I brainstormed by finding different styles, including line art, vector shapes, and various other creative treatments. Three styles were then shortlisted for the team's feedback. The vector style was chosen for its stronger consistency with the a11y logo design.

    Inspired by Universal Accessibility icons

    I opted for abstract human figures when defining the visual style, for it's simplicity to allow the viewer to project themselves onto the characters, ensuring the visuals resonated with everyone, regardless of disability. This resonates with the familiar and universally understood accessibility signs commonly encountered in public, with their simplified forms representing icons of accessibility.

    Elevating the copy with another layer of storytelling

    Each visual has a message to convey, such as themes of "Representation" and "Inclusivity", or even depicting pain points of the PWD users in scenarios where they are frustrated with digital services that are not accessible.

    Celebrating Diversity and Inclusivity

    In the hero section, I portrayed the idea of inclusivity through showing happy, diverse imagery of PWD users. This highlights the ultimate goal of a11y Playground: Making digital spaces accessible to everyone and, in turn, fostering user satisfaction.

    Converting sketches to High-Fidelity Vectors for Technical-Handover

    After many iterations, I created high-fidelity designs in Procreate using a11y logo colours and ensured they fit the layout of the website. These were converted to vector SVGs in Adobe Illustrator for efficient web loading and easy customisation (e.g. changing colour) by colleagues in Figma.

    Adding animation & exporting as Lottie JSON

    Animations were added in Adobe After Effects and exported as Lottie JSON files for the Software Engineer to use. This keeps the file size small while maintaining quality when upscaling.

    Ensuring Inclusive & Accessible Designs for all Users

    Contrast-driven hierarchy

    I utilised negative space to create distinct contrast between subjects to ensure the artwork is perfectly visible across both light and dark modes.

    Colour-inclusive vision

    Colour palettes were validated via colour blindness simulators to guarantee that the design remains clear and distinguishable for visually-impaired users.

    Descriptive alt-text

    Alt-text was integrated to bridge the gap for screen-reader users, ensuring that visual context is accessible to everyone.

    Sharing my process during Design Studio

    Finally, I was also given the chance to present my illustration process to over 30 UX designers during our monthly design studio. It felt meaningful to be sharing my process with everyone else, as some told me they had learned a thing or two from it. Most importantly, I also got to hone my presentation skills and learned how to integrate accessibility with my drawings.

    Building digital screens for Accessibility Checklist Articles

    I also assisted with other tasks, such as creating Figma frames for the "Principle of Accessibility: Top 10 Issues" pages. I designed these with a "large screen" first, given that the website is primarily viewed on laptops. The nature of this project focuses more on presenting informational content rather than complex user flows, so I prioritised a clear hierarchy with the different header sizes, as well as ensuring the content is easily digestible. All the content were taken from our previous write-up on notion, which I translated the content into frames here.

    (Had more experience doing up Figma screen flows & technical documentation in another project, but it's confidential)

    I also created picture infographics to visually explain the content of the examples in the articles, to make them more engaging and easier to understand for users. I added alt text to explain these images for screen-reader users, ensuring that the information is accessible to everyone.

    Discovering Insights through participating in user interviews and synthesising findings

    I collaborated with another UX designer on a user testing initiative to discover insights that could help improve our website's experience for people working in government (Software Developers, Product Managers, UX/UI Designers). I helped scribe the interviews (6 of them), meticulously documenting observed behaviours and identified pain points. Additionally, I took on the responsibility of conducting one of the user tests myself. This allowed me to directly observe and understand the user's experience with the website firsthand, gaining insights into their perception of our content's claritym and the website's overall ease of interaction.

    Hypothesis:

    💡 If current site structure is intuitive enough, such that role-based priorities do not hinder navigation to find key information and resources to integrate into their workflow.

    💡 Whether the website's information & content is accurate and easily understandable across different professional roles.

    Afterwards, I transcribed the interviews using Dovetail and synthesised key findings and insights, clustering them into different categories based on “content”, “features”, “positive feedback”, and “areas of confusion”, specifically for the checklist and resources sections. These findings were then entered into Google Sheets, with interview timestamps for a consolidated and clearer viewing which key insights were then derived from. Finally, each finding was labelled with it's severity issue (minor, moderate, major) and further labelled with the type of action (copy, design) needed to address it for the team.

    I got the opportunity to present the findings and recommendations to the rest of the team in a refined slide deck, which included statistics such as the number of participants who experienced confusion. From this, I also learned the importance of elaborating on the nature and context of their confusion, providing more detailed scenarios to the team for better understanding. The recommendations were used by the team for next steps to address the issues.

    Examples of some key findings & recommendations:

    🧠 Terminology comprehension varies by role, leading to inconsistent information interpretation. (e.g "site component" was interpreted differently by a SWE vs product manager).

    (Recommendation: Revise the terminology copy and add definitions for key terms for more clarification.)

    🌐 Professionals prioritise technical workflow integration over empathy-building when accessing information and resources as they are focused on working fast.

    (Recommendation: Reorder the flow of the sections in the homepage to move "Top 5 at-a-glance issues" above the "People's stories".)

    Overall, during this internship, a11y was one of the most meaningful projects I've worked on, as I got to learn about accessibility principles and the importance of designing for all users. My key takeaway was the importance of digital accessibility, as it highlighted the necessity of designing with a user-centric approach, ensuring that every individual has a smooth and equitable digital experience, including PWDs. It was also meaningful to be creating tech for the public good, as I got to improve people's lives through my daily work.
