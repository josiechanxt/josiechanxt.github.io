Between Past and Progress:
A walk from Chinatown to Tanjong Pagar

Data Collection & Publication

School Project • 2024

Project Brief

This project was part of the “Visualising The City” module, where students are required to conduct physical fieldwork and research to gather data for a final visualisation.

  • Under the guidance of Qimmy Lim

  • Timeline: 11 Weeks

  • Collaborator(s): Solo Project

  • Key Skills: Data Collection, Publication Design, Storytelling

    • My Interpretation

    Singapore began as a modest trading port attracting diverse settlers and has transformed into a vibrant modern city over the past 50 years. This evolution has inspired me to explore Singapore's journey from its historical roots to its contemporary identity. To do so, I focused on two distinct areas: Chinatown, a historic district known for its traditional shophouses, temples and bustling markets, and Tanjong Pagar’s modern CBD district with many offices and skyscrapers.

    Objective

    By journeying along a route between these two contrasting areas, the observed surroundings and building materials serve as an insightful lens to document Singapore's transition from 'Old' (traditional) to 'New' (modern).

    Data Collection Method

    Photography was used to authentically collect data. To portray the area's age, I decided to focus on materials such as the building's structure, wall texture, and tiles, as these elements effectively depict its inherent rawness.

    Field Works & Research

    Phase 1: Data Collection, Fieldworks

    Fieldwork sessions were conducted in Chinatown and Tanjong Pagar, where I explored routes, photographed tiles, and analysed the surroundings to understand Singapore's evolution into a modern Garden City. A highlight was joining a Chinatown tour, where I learned about hidden sights, how people lived in the past, and the city’s growth from my tour guide.

    Phase 2: Organising Data

    Using 'Chinatown Street Market' as an example, the collected data was organised in a grid format for clearer viewing, arranged in chronological order based on the pictures taken. Photos of the buildings, as well as the textures of walls and tiles, were also documented.

    Phase 3a: Synthesising Data

    The texture of the walls was then omitted for a simpler and more effective presentation, particularly in the form of a book. Sometimes, simplicity conveys the message more effectively.

    Phase 3b: Synthesising Data

    Metadata is also crucial for adding context to the photos and making the data more complete. Details such as the names of each respective point were identified using the image data feature in the Photos app, geotagged via Google Maps. The condition of the tiles at each location was then analysed accordingly.

    Book Content

    Overall Data | Section A

    In total, there are 68 points and tiles, and this data will be useful in Section A of the book set, where it features the transition from 'old' to 'new' from Chinatown to Tanjong Pagar through the route.

    Overall Data | Section B

    While following the route to collect data, I noticed some restrictions. However, by observing my surroundings closely, I discovered insights into the intersection of 'old' and 'new', revealing Singapore's approach to urban planning. I also learned about conservation efforts and Singapore's heritage, deepening my understanding of how Singapore has evolved from a humble trading port to a modern city.

    Execution: Prototyping, book design, user experience

    Art Direction of books

    The book's art direction revolves around squares, inspired by the shape of tiles and represented as colour-coded icons for seamless interaction between the two books. The typewriter font and textured pages provide a minimalistic, clean base, enhancing the tactile experience while drawing attention to the photos and tiles through their vibrant contrast.

    Prototyping

    Multiple physical prototypes were created throughout the process for proofreading and to test the interaction aspects. This made spotting errors that might have gone unnoticed digitally easier, especially with the tab elements, which can be difficult to visualise online.

    User Experience

    Multiple physical prototypes were created throughout the process for proofreading and to Initially, Sections A and B were combined into one book, but users found it challenging to navigate. The prototypes helped me realise that splitting them into separate books would provide a better user experience. Additionally, my previous prototype was a lot smaller, making it tough to read, which prompted me to increase it's size.

    Making of books

    I printed the pages from InDesign using the "Print Booklets" option to create the signatures in preparation for binding. I also hand-cut everything for better accuracy, especially since I had to account for the tabs. While there were many technical aspects to consider, which made the process a bit tedious, it was definitely rewarding when the book set finally came together~

    Final Book Set

    My final visualisation is an interactive two-book set (Section A: Tiles Documentation, Section B: Context to the Areas), featuring tabs and coloured icons for cross-referencing Singapore’s history and modernity.
